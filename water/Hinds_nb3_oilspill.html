Hydrocarbons in the marine environment¶

Synthesizing the readings (Ch. 4 in Weis) and lecture, provide a short introduction to hydrocarbons in the marine environment. In particular, describe the main characteristics that affect the fate of hydrocarbons (viscosity, volatility, and weight).

Natural Hydrocarbons are organic chemical compounds which form the base of important energy sources such as crude oil, natrual gas, and coal. They are created as a result of planktonic photosynthesis. Preservation of these hydrocarbons can be dependant on high sediment deposition rates and low bottom water dissolved oxygen, in which case they can be buried before oxidized or decomposed. Viscosity, flow potential, is impacted by tmeperature and pressure applied to the hydrocarbon as it changes the compound shape and density. Volatility, vaporization rate, is influenced by intermolecular forces which are impacted by the photosynthesis process. Weight of hydrocarbons depend on the continuous build up of molecular matter. These characterstics can be tracked by assessing the accumulation network, which include hysrocarbon source, accumulation terminal, the pathway connecting the termai and source, and the migration method potential. After a few human lifetimes, if not more, these hydrocarbons are compacted enough to be used as the energy sources the modern world heavily depend on.

Circulation in the Santa Barbara Channel¶

There are three synpotic patterns of circulation in the Santa Barbara Channel: upwelling, convergent, and relaxation. The basic ideas of this were laid out in Harms & Winant, 1998 (see Fig. 10) and then considered in further detail by Winant et al 2013 (see Fig. 5, top row). (The cyclonic eddy in the 1998 paper is termed convergent in the 2003 paper).

Summarize these three states in a short narrative, including the typical season that these flow states tend to most frequently occur.

Upwelling:

Usually consists of equatorward flow from surface level to 45m depth, except in the area in Santa Barbara County that is adjacent to the main coastline. This pattern occurs mostly during Spring, March and April, when winds are upwelling avorable and pressure at the surface is constant.

Convergent:

Occurs during Summer, July-September, and consists of equatorward surface flow, and poleward flow at 45m depth. As a result of the flow convergence, the water body moves mostly in a offshore or westward direction. Winds during this pattern are strong and upwelling favorable and the variable upwelling has created surface pressure gradients alongshore.

Relaxation:

Often occuring during Winter, November-December. Water currents experience mostly polward flow from surface to 45m depth. In the Santa Barbara channel, much of this water can get trapped as poleward flow clashes with the majority of the coastline.

Variability

The WebGnome model utilizes the changing variables of Wind Speed (kts) and Wind Direction (deg), and consistent variables of a 5-day timeline and 160 barrel/day volume. The Upwelling and Convergent models transport the oil across a large spatial extent. These models differed in both wind speed (11kt vs. 8kt) and direction (315deg vs. 260deg.), but still managed to transport extensively. The Relaxation model included winds at 9kts and 200 deg., but covered the least amount of water, and pressed mostly against the coastline neartest to the spill sight. Upwelling patterns tend to shift waters downcoast (equatorward), convergent patterns tend to circulate due to interfering currents, and relaxation patterns shift waters upcoast (poleward). As a result in this example, the relaxation cycle seeps into the land more densely than the other two patterns.
